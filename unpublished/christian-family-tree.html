The Christian Family Tree

Tracing the trunk, the branches — and the plant that claims to share the same roots but doesn't.

Many visitors to this site come assuming that Catholicism, Eastern Orthodoxy, Protestantism, and Latter-day Saint Christianity are all roughly equivalent expressions of the same Christian tradition — branches of the same tree, differing mainly in emphasis or practice. This assumption is historically and theologically incorrect, and the differences are not minor.

The diagram below visualizes what the historical record actually shows: a single trunk of Catholic Christianity traceable from Pentecost through an unbroken apostolic succession, from which two major branches split away at identifiable historical moments (AD 1054 and AD 1517). The Latter-day Saint church of 1830 is emphatically not a branch of this tree — it explicitly claims the entire tree had died fourteen centuries earlier, and that Joseph Smith planted an entirely new one from different seed. That claim has severe evidential consequences explored throughout this site.

The Branches Explained
✝
The Trunk: Catholic Christianity
AD 33 — Present

The Church founded by Christ at Pentecost, entrusted to the Apostles, and governed through their successors. The Catholic claim is not that it is one Christian tradition among many, but that it is the original and continuous body — identical in institutional identity to the Church of Ignatius of Antioch (AD 107), the Council of Nicaea (AD 325), and every era since. Its unbroken episcopal succession is historically documentable from Peter to the present.

☦
Eastern Orthodoxy
AD 1054 · Great Schism

The Eastern Orthodox churches separated from Rome in the Great Schism of 1054, primarily over the Filioque clause and papal jurisdiction. Critically, Eastern Orthodoxy is a genuine branch — it retains valid apostolic succession, the seven sacraments, and the same patristic tradition. The disagreement with Rome is real and serious; the shared roots are equally real. This is the key distinction from Protestantism and especially from LDS Christianity.

✟
Protestant Denominations
AD 1517 · Reformation

Beginning with Luther's 95 Theses in 1517, Protestant traditions emerged as reforming movements within or departing from the Catholic Church. They retain significant elements from the trunk — the Trinitarian creed, the biblical canon, the two-nature Christology of Chalcedon — while rejecting others (papal authority, the sacrificial Mass, the full sacramental system). They are genuine branches, even if severed ones: their theology is shaped entirely by engagement with and reaction to Catholic Christianity.

≠
LDS: Not a Branch
Founded 1830 · Separate Soil

The LDS church does not merely reform the trunk — it denies the trunk ever survived. Joseph Smith taught that God told him in 1820 that all existing Christian churches "were all wrong" and "all their creeds were an abomination" (JS–H 1:19). This is not a branch position but a complete rejection of the tree's survival. LDS theology also differs at root level: a corporeal, progressing God (not the eternal Trinity), a pre-mortal existence, and an entirely different plan of salvation requiring temple ordinances.

✦   Why the Distinction Matters   ✦

When LDS missionaries describe their church as a "restored" Christianity, the word "restored" carries an enormous theological claim that is rarely made explicit: that the entire continuous history of Christianity — every martyr, every council, every patristic theologian, every faithful bishop from AD 100 to AD 1830 — was not merely imperfect but part of a fraudulent apostate institution. Tertullian, Ignatius, Augustine, Aquinas, and every bishop in the Eastern and Western churches were either deceived or participants in a universal corruption so total that God Himself abandoned the Church Christ had promised the gates of Hell would not overcome.

The Catholic response is not primarily rhetorical — it is historical. The family tree above is not an assertion; it is a record. The apostolic succession is documentable. The Church Fathers wrote extensively and their writings survive. The councils are dated, minuted, and attributable. If the entire tree died, someone needs to explain where, exactly, the life went out of it — and why the evidence uniformly points the opposite direction.
