Thai Tones

Thai has 5 tones. The same syllable with a different tone means something completely different. This is the key to being understood!

กา
Mid Tone (สามัญ)
Flat, neutral pitch — like stating a fact calmly. กา (gaa) = crow
ก่า
Low Tone (เอก)
Pitch drops low and stays — like a disappointed "oh."
ก้า
Falling Tone (โท)
Starts high, drops sharply — like an emphatic "No!"
ก๊า
High Tone (ตรี)
Pitch rises high — like an excited question.
ก๋า
Rising Tone (จัตวา)
Dips low then rises — like a surprised "Really?"

Classic example: "ไหม" — mái (rising) = new, mâi (falling) = not, máai (high) = silk, mǎai (rising) = wood. Your wife can help you hear the differences!

Survival Tips

🙏 ครับ / ค่ะ — Polite Particles

As a male, add ครับ (kráp) to the end of sentences. Your wife uses ค่ะ (kâ). This is the single easiest way to sound polite and respectful — use it with everyone.

😊 Smile — It Goes Far

Thailand is called the "Land of Smiles." Even rough Thai + a smile goes a very long way. Thai people genuinely appreciate any effort to speak their language.

🌶️ Ordering Food Hack

Say ไม่เผ็ด (mâi pèt) for "not spicy" or เผ็ดนิดหน่อย (pèt nít nòi) for "a little spicy." Thai spicy is no joke!

🗣️ No Verb Conjugation!

Thai verbs never change for tense, person, or number. "Go" is always ไป (bpai). Context and time words do the heavy lifting.

👑 Pronouns Are Flexible

Use ผม (pǒm) for "I/me" as a male. With your wife, use เธอ (ter) for "you" informally. In casual speech, pronouns are often dropped entirely.

🔢 555 = LOL

The number 5 in Thai is ห้า (hâa). So 555 sounds like "hahaha" — Thai people text this instead of "LOL."

🙏 The Wai

The traditional Thai greeting — palms pressed together at chest level with a slight bow. The higher the hands, the more respect shown. Don't wai to children or service staff — they'll wai you first.
